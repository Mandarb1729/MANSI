Our Inspirations

Srinivas Ramanujan


Srinivasa Ramanujan Aiyangar FRS (22 December 1887 – 26 April 1920) was an Indian mathematician. Though he had almost no formal training in pure mathematics, he made substantial contributions to mathematical analysis, number theory, infinite series, and continued fractions, including solutions to mathematical problems then considered unsolvable. Ramanujan was born in Erode, India, in 1887. He showed an early aptitude for mathematics, but his formal education was interrupted by poverty and illness. In 1913, he began sending his mathematical work to G. H. Hardy, a British mathematician at Cambridge University. Hardy was immediately impressed by Ramanujan's genius and invited him to come to England. ,In England, Ramanujan collaborated with Hardy and other mathematicians at Cambridge. He made significant contributions to a wide range of mathematical topics, including number theory, infinite series, and continued fractions. His work was often characterized by its originality and insight, and he is considered one of the greatest mathematicians of the 20th century. Ramanujan died in Madras, India, in 1920, at the age of 32. He was a devout Hindu and his life was guided by his religious beliefs. He was a deeply spiritual man and his mathematical work was often inspired by his religious faith. Ramanujan's legacy is immense. His work has had a profound impact on the development of mathematics, and his ideas continue to inspire mathematicians today. He is a true mathematical genius, and his life and work are an inspiration to us all.

Prashantchandra Mahalanobis

Prasanta Chandra Mahalanobis (OBE, FNA, FASc, FRS) (29 June 1893 – 28 June 1972) was an Indian scientist and statistician. He is best known for the Mahalanobis distance, a statistical measure, and for founding the Indian Statistical Institute. Mahalanobis was born in Calcutta (Kolkata), India, in 1893. He studied physics at the University of Calcutta, but he was more interested in mathematics and statistics. After graduating from Calcutta, he went to Cambridge University in England, where he studied mathematics and statistics with some of the leading mathematicians of the time. In 1921, Mahalanobis returned to India and founded the Indian Statistical Institute (ISI) in Calcutta. The ISI quickly became a leading center for statistical research and training. Mahalanobis himself made significant contributions to a wide range of statistical topics, including sampling theory, multivariate analysis, and statistical inference. Mahalanobis was also a pioneer in the application of statistics to a wide range of practical problems, including agriculture, public health, and industrial planning. He was a member of the first Planning Commission of India, and he played a key role in the development of India's Five-Year Plans. Mahalanobis was a Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) and the National Academy of Sciences (NAS). He was awarded the Padma Vibhushan, India's second highest civilian honor, in 1966. Mahalanobis is considered one of the most important statisticians of the 20th century. He made significant contributions to the development of statistical theory and methods, and he played a key role in the development of statistics in India.
